Lecture 1

  1. Block diagram:
    • It represents flow of information.
    • Blocks are systems. They have equations and are represented by blocks.
    • Inputs and outputs of blocks are signal. They usually have units, are functions of time and are represented by the connections(arrows).

Lecture 2

  1. Linear system:

    • $f(u_{1})+f(u_{2})=f(u_{1}+u_{2})$

    • Satisfy the principal of superposition.

    • Casual: output at $t$ only depends on inputs before $t$ but not inputs after $t$.

    • Time-invariant: as long as values of inputs are the same, values of outputs will be same. Independent of when the input is applied.

    • If a system can be described by a linear differential equation with constant coefficients, the system must be linear.

    • We can approximate a system to be linear for small perturbations from equilibrium(摄动). See the example below, we assume $\dot x=0$ at $(x_{0},y_{0})$, $x = x_{0}+\delta{x}$ and $y = y_{0}+\delta{y}$

      • $\because \dot{x}=f(x,y)$

      • $\therefore \dot{x_{0}}=f(x_{0},y_{0})=0$

        $\dot{x_{0}}+\dot{\delta x} = f(x_{0}+\delta{x},y_{0}+\delta{y})$

      • Using Taylor expansion

        $\dot{\delta x} = f(x_{0},y_{0})+\frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial x}(x_{0}+\delta x - x_{0})+\frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial y}(y_{0}+\delta y - y_{0})$

      • $\dot{\delta{x}} = \frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial x}\delta x+\frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial y}\delta y$

      • $\dot{\delta x}=A\delta{x}+B\delta{y}$

Lecture 3

​

  1. Laplace transform revision:

    • Definition:
      $$
      G(s)=\mathcal{L}(g(t))=\int_{0}^{\infty}g(t)\cdot(e^{-st})dt
      $$

    • Linearity: the operation of taking Laplace transform is linear.
      $$
      \mathcal{L}(\alpha g_{1}(t)+\beta g_{2}(t))=\mathcal{L}(\alpha g_{1}(t))+\mathcal{L}(\beta g_{2}(t))
      $$

  • Useful tricks:

    • shift in $s$:
      $$
      \mathcal{L}(e^{-\alpha t}g(t)) = G(s+\alpha)
      $$

    • Multiply by $t$:
      $$
      \mathcal{L}(t\cdot g(t))=\frac {d}{ds}(G(s))
      $$

    • Shift in $t$:
      $$
      \mathcal{L}(g(t-T))=e^{-sT}G(s)
      $$

    • Convolution:
      $$
      \mathcal{L}(\int ^{t}_{0} f(\tau)g(t-\tau)d\tau) =\mathcal{L}(f(t))\cdot \mathcal{L}(g(t))
      $$

  1. Transform of derivatives: differentiation(in the t domain) corresponds to multiply by s(in the s domain).

  2. Integration of derivatives: integration(in the t domain) corresponds to divide by s(in the s domain).

  3. Final value theorems:
    $$
    \lim _{t \rightarrow \infty} y(t)=\lim _{s \rightarrow 0} s \bar{y}(s)
    $$

  4. Initial value theorems:
    $$
    \lim _{t \rightarrow 0^{+}} y(t)=\lim _{s \rightarrow \infty} s \bar{y}(s)
    $$

Lecture 4

  1. Transfer function($G(s)$):
    • $Y(s)=G(s)\cdot U(s)$ where $Y(s)$ is the Laplace transform of the output and $U(s)$ is the Laplace transform of the input.
    • $\mathcal{L}(y(t))=\mathcal{L}(\int ^{t}_{0} f(\tau)g(t-\tau)d\tau) =\mathcal{L}(u(t))\cdot \mathcal{L}(g(t))$ states that Laplace transform of the output equals the Laplace transform of input times the Laplace transform of the impulse response
    • As a result $\mathcal{L}(g(t))=G(s)$ Laplace transform of the impulse response is the transfer function.
    • We could get the transfer function by finding the impulse response but it is generally easier to find transfer function from differential equations.
    • Laplace transform simplifies calculation by turning a convolution to a multiplication.

Notes from Supervision 1

  1. There is always a input signal(usually desired value) and a output signal(usually true value). Output is subtracted from the input leaving error.
  2. When doing the inverse of the Laplace transform, always multiply expression by $H(t)$ or state $t>0$
